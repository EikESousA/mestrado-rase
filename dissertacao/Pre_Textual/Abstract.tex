\begin{resumo}[Abstract]
 \begin{otherlanguage*}{english}
    The construction industry still spends substantial time manually checking project drawings against technical standards. Building Information Modeling (BIM) has centralized project data, yet automatic compliance checking remains blocked by an old obstacle: standards are written in natural language, with ambiguity and cross-references. One alternative proposed in the literature is the RASE methodology (\textit{Requirement, Applicability, Selection, Exception}), which rewrites standards as four logical operators amenable to computational verification. The catch is that this conversion is still performed by hand. Large Language Models (LLMs) emerge as natural candidates to automate that translation, but systematic evaluations of open-source alternatives applied to Brazilian Portuguese are scarce. This dissertation fills that gap by comparing six open-source LLMs --- Llama, Dolphin, Gemma, Mistral, Alpaca, and Qwen --- on the conversion of AEC technical standards to the RASE format using \textbf{Prompt Engineering only} (no Fine-Tuning, no Retrieval-Augmented Generation). The conversion is broken into three levels: N1 segments the normative sentence into atomic rules; N2 identifies the RASE operators in each rule (Requirement, Applicability, Selection, and Exception); and N3 maps each operator to a structured JSON. Three experiments were carried out --- EN1 (N1 in isolation), EN2 (N2 in isolation, fed by the reference N1), and EN1N2 (chained pipeline) ---, together with an isolated assessment of N3, in order to quantify error propagation along the chain. The six models were served locally via Ollama on the same hardware and applied to a dataset of 79 annotated Brazilian standards, derived from NBR~9050. The assessment combined thirteen metrics over five families: lexical similarity (FuzzyWuzzy, TF-IDF); semantic similarity (SBERT, BERTimbau, Multilingual); distance metrics (Word Mover's Distance with FastText and NILC embeddings); generation-oriented metrics (BERTScore, ROUGE-L); and classification metrics (Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score). Dolphin finished with the best global average (0.676) across the nine similarity metrics and the three experiments, ahead of Llama (0.658) and Mistral (0.654). Beyond raw quality, Dolphin delivered the best cost-benefit ratio: it runs about ninety times faster than Llama in the chained pipeline. Llama, in turn, dominated EN2 and N3-isolated on the semantic metrics. Among the metrics, semantic ones and BERTScore proved more informative than the lexical ones; BERTimbau, trained on a Brazilian Portuguese legal corpus, kept a consistent edge over SBERT. The classification metrics exposed a common weak spot: Selection and Exception score F1 close to zero in N2 for every model, signalling a structural limitation of the current prompt for those two operators. As contributions, this research provides: an operational definition of the three RASE normalization levels, a reproducible set of prompts by level and by operator, and the first systematic comparison of six open-source LLMs over Brazilian AEC standards.

   \noindent
   \textbf{Keywords}: Machine Learning, Natural Language Processing, LLM, Prompt Engineering, BIM, RASE.
 \end{otherlanguage*}
\end{resumo}
